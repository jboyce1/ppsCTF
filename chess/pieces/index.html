Cybersecurity Chess

Pawn (1 point)

Basic reconnaissance: Involves initial gathering of information about the target network. Identifies open ports, services, and subdomains without aggressive interaction.

Scanning and enumeration: More in-depth analysis to discover vulnerabilities and weaknesses in the network's defenses, like outdated software or unprotected entry points.

Knight (3 points)

Initial access: Achieving user-level access to a host or non-critical system, often through exploiting known vulnerabilities or social engineering tactics.

Lateral movement: Spreading across the network from the initial entry point, seeking higher privileges or more sensitive data, without yet gaining full control.

Bishop (5 points)

Privileged access: Gaining higher-level permissions, potentially including administrator rights, to access more critical systems or data.

Data exfiltration: Transferring sensitive data from the target network to the attacker, often stealthily to avoid detection.

Rook (7 points)

Persistence: Establishing mechanisms to maintain access over time, often through backdoors or other covert methods.

Escalation of privileges: Expanding access rights, often by exploiting system flaws, to gain higher level of control over network resources.

Queen (10 points)

Root access: Achieving the highest level of system access, allowing complete control over the network, akin to the most powerful chess piece.

Complete control: Gaining authority over critical systems, which can include altering or halting essential operations.

Checkmate (15 points)

Complete compromise: Achieving full dominion over the target network, including root access, data theft, and the ability to disrupt operations, representing a decisive victory.

Position (Bonus Points)

Mate in 3 (5 bonus points): Securing a strategic position within the network, such as root privileges on an admin account or running command-and-control operations, reflecting a significant tactical advantage.
